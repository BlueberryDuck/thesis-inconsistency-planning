\section{Introduction and Motivation}

In practical applications of automated planning, systems regularly encounter unsolvable problems. A planning instance is unsolvable if no sequence of available actions can transform the initial state into a goal state~\cite{Russell2016}. Since determining solvability is PSPACE-complete~\cite{Bylander1994}, unsolvable instances arise naturally in complex domains rather than being merely edge cases. Consider a logistics scenario where a delivery company needs to route packages through a network of distribution centers. If the problem specifies that a package must be delivered to New York by noon while simultaneously requiring it to be processed at a facility in Los Angeles at the same time, the planning system will simply report "unsolvable" without explaining the conflicting requirements~\cite{Goebelbecker2010,Sreedharan2019}. Similarly, in robotics applications, a robot might be asked to manipulate objects in ways that violate physical constraints, or in manufacturing, production schedules might contain contradictory resource requirements.

Beyond recognizing unsolvability, users require explanations of why the planner failed to find a solution. Current planning systems excel at finding solutions when they exist, but provide minimal diagnostic information when problems are unsolvable~\cite{Eriksson2018,Sreedharan2019}. This limitation becomes particularly problematic in complex domains where problems are automatically generated or when domain experts need to understand why their specifications cannot be satisfied~\cite{Sreedharan2019}. Manual debugging of such failures is impractical for large-scale problems with hundreds of constraints and actions, yet current approaches offer no systematic alternatives~\cite{Goebelbecker2010,Sreedharan2019}. The lack of quantitative diagnostic capabilities forces users into costly trial-and-error processes, significantly hindering the practical deployment of automated planning in real-world applications~\cite{Eriksson2018}.

The field of inconsistency measurement in knowledge representation and reasoning~\cite{Thimm2019} offers a promising approach to address this limitation. Rather than treating inconsistency as a binary property, these techniques quantify the degree of inconsistency within logical knowledge bases. A multitude of measures have been introduced, each capturing different aspects of logical conflicts. Minimal inconsistent and maximal consistent set-based measures~\cite{Hunter2008}, distance-based measures~\cite{Grant2013,Grant2017}, hitting-set measures~\cite{Thimm2016}, contension-based measures~\cite{Grant2011,Thimm2016}, and forgetting-based measures~\cite{Besnard2016} have all been developed to provide formal methods for quantifying the severity of inconsistencies. These measures enable comparative analysis and systematic approaches to conflict resolution, allowing users to identify which parts of a knowledge base contribute most significantly to its inconsistency~\cite{Thimm2019}.

However, while these approaches have been primarily applied to logical knowledge bases and have recently been adapted to other domains such as declarative process specifications~\cite{Corea2022} and temporal reasoning~\cite{Condotta2024}, their systematic adaptation to planning domains remains largely unexplored. Planning problems present unique challenges for inconsistency measurement since, unlike propositional knowledge bases, planning involves sequences of actions that transform states over time~\cite{Russell2016}. Conflicts can arise not only from contradictory static constraints but also from action sequences that lead to impossible state combinations or from goals that cannot be reached given the available actions.

This research aims to bridge inconsistency measurement and planning by exploring how techniques from knowledge representation and reasoning can be adapted to quantify the degree of inconsistency in unsolvable planning instances. The thesis develops quantitative diagnostics that characterize which goals or constraints drive a task's unsolvability. The approach focuses on \ac{ASP}-based algorithms~\cite{Ulbricht2016}, which are well-suited for this task due to their declarative modeling capabilities and support for temporal and combinatorial constraints~\cite{Dimopoulos1997,Dimopoulos2019}. Recent work demonstrates that \ac{ASP}-based approaches generally outperform \ac{SAT}-based methods for computing inconsistency measures~\cite{Kuhlmann2025}, supporting the choice of \ac{ASP} for this domain adaptation. If successful, such quantitative measures could provide systematic approaches to analyzing and fixing planning problems, filling a critical gap in current planning methodology.

\section{Technical Foundations and Related Work}

This section establishes the theoretical foundations by examining inconsistency measurement techniques, classical planning formalisms, and existing work connecting the domains.

\subsection{Inconsistency Measurement in Knowledge Representation and Reasoning}

Inconsistency measurement provides formal methods for quantifying the degree of inconsistency within logical knowledge bases. Rather than treating inconsistency as a binary property, these measures assign numerical values that reflect the severity or extent of conflicts, enabling comparative analysis and systematic approaches to conflict resolution~\cite{Thimm2019}. A multitude of measures have been introduced, each capturing different aspects of logical conflicts and providing users with tools to identify which parts of a knowledge base contribute most significantly to its inconsistency~\cite{Thimm2018}.

Minimal inconsistent and maximal consistent set-based measures focus on the structural properties of conflicts, by counting or weighting minimal inconsistent and maximal consistent subsets to analyze how formulas participate in conflicts~\cite{Hunter2008}. Distance-based measures treat inconsistency as the distance from consistency, employing concepts such as Dalal distance to quantify the minimal modifications required to restore consistency~\cite{Grant2013,Grant2017}. Hitting-set measures seek the smallest set of interpretations such that each formula is satisfied by at least one interpretation~\cite{Thimm2016}. Contension-based measures employ paraconsistent or many-valued logics, building on Priest's logic of paradox~\cite{Priest1979} which introduces a third truth value \emph{B} for "both true and false", counting the minimal number of atoms that must be assigned this value to render the knowledge base consistent under paraconsistent semantics~\cite{Grant2011,Thimm2016}. Forgetting-based measures determine the minimal set of atoms that must be removed from consideration to restore consistency, providing insights into which propositions are central to conflicts~\cite{Besnard2016}.

While originally developed for logical knowledge bases, inconsistency measurement techniques have also been adapted to other domains. Notably \cite{Corea2022} has developed time-sensitive inconsistency measures for temporal logic applied to process specifications, where traditional measures cannot adequately handle temporal operators or distinguish conflicts that affect different numbers of future states. Similarly, \cite{Condotta2024} has introduced a comprehensive framework for measuring inconsistency in temporal constraint problems, developing both adaptations of classical measures and temporal-specific approaches based on constraint relaxation. These domain adaptations demonstrate the potential for inconsistency measurement beyond classical logic.

Planning applications require computational efficiency and compatibility with non-monotonic reasoning systems. Experimental comparisons demonstrate that \ac{ASP}-based algorithms often outperform \ac{SAT}-based approaches for computing inconsistency measures~\cite{Kuhlmann2025}, making \ac{ASP} a promising foundation for planning applications. The theoretical groundwork for \ac{ASP}-based planning was established by~\cite{Dimopoulos1997}, with modern tools like plasp 3~\cite{Dimopoulos2019} providing efficient translations from \ac{PDDL} to \ac{ASP}. Importantly, inconsistency measures for \ac{ASP} have been developed~\cite{Ulbricht2016}, with the crucial observation that the monotonicity postulate does not hold in non-monotonic logics, a consideration directly relevant for planning domains with their frame problem and default assumptions about action effects.

\subsection{Classical Planning}

Classical planning operates on a state-based formalism where the world is represented as a set of propositions that can be true or false in any given state. Actions are defined by their preconditions and effects: what must be true for the action to be applicable, and how the action changes the world state~\cite{Russell2016}. A planning problem consists of three components: an initial state describing the starting configuration, a set of available actions, and a goal specification defining the desired outcome. The planning task is to find a sequence of actions that, when executed from the initial state, results in a state satisfying the goal conditions~\cite{Ghallab2004}.

Planning problems become unsolvable when no sequence of applicable actions can transform the initial state into a goal state. This can occur for several reasons: goals that are mutually exclusive and require contradictory states, required resources that are unavailable or insufficient, action preconditions that can never be satisfied, or action sequences that lead to dead-end states from which the goal cannot be reached. Determining whether a plan exists is PSPACE-complete~\cite{Bylander1994}, which has motivated extensive research into efficient algorithms and search techniques.

The \ac{STRIPS} representation, originally developed by~\cite{Fikes1971}, is foundational for many modern planning systems, providing a simple yet powerful framework for modeling planning domains where actions have deterministic effects and the world is fully observable.

\ac{PDDL} serves as the standard language for describing planning problems and is used in international planning competitions~\cite{McDermott1998,Ghallab1998}. \ac{PDDL} provides a structured way to specify domains and problems, making it essential for practical applications. However, given the complexity of \ac{PDDL}'s various extensions and features, this thesis initially focuses on \ac{STRIPS}-based formulations due to their foundational simplicity and well-understood semantics, which allow for systematic adaptation of inconsistency measures without the complications of advanced \ac{PDDL} constructs.

Modern planning systems employ various search strategies to explore different ways of reaching goals, such as searching forward from the initial state, working backward from the goal, or examining the space of possible plans~\cite{Ghallab2004,Russell2016}. Important algorithmic advances include techniques that build graphical representations of planning problems to identify conflicts and estimate solution difficulty~\cite{Blum1997}, and smart search methods like \ac{HSP}~\cite{Bonet2001} and \ac{FF}~\cite{Hoffmann2001} that use simplified versions of problems to guide their search toward solutions. These heuristic approaches have significantly improved planning performance for solvable instances, but when these systems encounter an unsolvable problem, they typically provide minimal diagnostic information beyond reporting failure~\cite{Eriksson2018}. This diagnostic gap represents a significant limitation for users attempting to understand the source of conflicts or determine appropriate modifications to achieve solvability.

\subsection{Inconsistency and Planning: Foundational Work}

The connection between inconsistency and planning has been explored from several perspectives, with foundational work establishing important theoretical and diagnostic approaches for unsolvable planning problems. While the broader field of inconsistency measurement in knowledge representation and reasoning has developed various techniques for quantifying conflicts in logical knowledge bases~\cite{Thimm2019,Grant2023}, their systematic adaptation to planning domains remains an open research direction.

Important theoretical groundwork on proof systems for demonstrating unsolvability in planning has been established by~\cite{Eriksson2018}. This work proposes a rule-based proof system for proving unsolvability in planning, examining why certain planning tasks cannot be solved using \ac{STRIPS}-based approaches. While this research provides systematic approaches to proving unsolvability, it focuses on generating verifiable certificates rather than measuring degrees of inconsistency.

From a diagnostic perspective, \cite{Goebelbecker2010} introduced "excuses" for unsolvable planning tasks, minimal modifications required to restore solvability, such as removing specific goals or constraints. While conceptually similar to minimal inconsistent sets in logical knowledge bases~\cite{Hunter2008}, excuses provide only binary guidance rather than quantitative measures of inconsistency severity, lacking comparative analysis capabilities for prioritizing repair strategies.

Complementing these diagnostic approaches, methods for producing explanations that help users understand why planning tasks are unsolvable have been developed by~\cite{Sreedharan2019}, focusing on the identification of unreachable subgoals. This approach emphasizes explanatory aspects rather than quantitative measurement, but demonstrates the practical value of systematic analysis when planning problems are unsolvable. While valuable for understanding individual cases of unsolvability, these explanation methods do not provide the quantitative measures needed for systematic problem analysis, automated repair, or comparative evaluation of different unsolvable instances.

These research efforts establish important theoretical and practical foundations for understanding unsolvable planning problems. However, they reveal a fundamental limitation: existing approaches primarily focus on binary characterizations of unsolvability rather than quantitative analysis. The proof systems of~\cite{Eriksson2018} verify unsolvability, the excuses of~\cite{Goebelbecker2010} identify minimal repairs, and the explanations of~\cite{Sreedharan2019} clarify unreachable goals, but none provide the comparative quantitative measures needed for systematic analysis across different unsolvable instances. This thesis builds on these foundations by adapting inconsistency measurement techniques from knowledge representation and reasoning to provide the missing quantitative dimension for planning diagnostics.

\section{Problem Statement}

Current planning systems provide minimal diagnostic information when encountering unsolvable problems. Users lack insight into why problems are unsolvable and have no systematic way to analyze the structure of unsolvable instances. This makes it difficult to identify minimal changes that could restore solvability, as different types of unsolvable planning problems are not distinguished or quantified. The lack of quantitative diagnostic capabilities forces users into trial-and-error processes, significantly limiting the practical applicability of automated planning in complex real-world scenarios.

\section{Research Questions and Objectives}

The central research question of this thesis is:

\begin{quote}
    How can inconsistency measures from knowledge representation and reasoning be meaningfully adapted to classical planning to provide diagnostic value for unsolvable planning problems?
\end{quote}

This central question is addressed through the following research questions and their corresponding objectives:

\subsection{Research Question 1: Theoretical Adaptation}

\begin{quote}
    Which inconsistency measures from knowledge representation and reasoning can be adapted to classical planning, and what postulates do they retain?
\end{quote}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item[(1a)] Survey existing inconsistency measures from knowledge representation and reasoning.
    \item[(1b)] Analyze the structural differences between knowledge bases and planning problems, identifying adaptation challenges.
    \item[(1c)] Develop formal adaptations of 2-3 selected inconsistency measures to \ac{STRIPS}-based planning formulations.
    \item[(1d)] Examine which postulates are preserved or modified in the planning context.
    \item[(1e)] Provide formal definitions and examples of the adapted measures.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Research Question 2: Computational Implementation}

\begin{quote}
    How can the adapted measures be efficiently computed for planning problems using Answer Set Programming?
\end{quote}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item[(2a)] Design \ac{ASP}-based encodings for the adapted inconsistency measures.
    \item[(2b)] Develop algorithms that handle standard planning problem formats, including \ac{STRIPS} and optionally basic \ac{PDDL} representations.
    \item[(2c)] Implement a prototype system that can parse planning problems and compute the adapted measures.
    \item[(2d)] Analyze computational complexity and if possible optimize \ac{ASP} encodings for scalability in planning domains.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Research Question 3: Practical Evaluation (Optional)}

\begin{quote}
    Do the adapted measures provide practical diagnostic value for understanding and analyzing unsolvable planning problems?
\end{quote}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item[(3a)] Establish an experimental framework using benchmark planning problems from established collections.
    \item[(3b)] Evaluate computational feasibility and scalability of the implemented measures on problems of varying complexity.
    \item[(3c)] Analyze the diagnostic utility of the measures for distinguishing different types of unsolvable planning problems.
    \item[(3d)] Assess whether the measures provide actionable insights for problem repair and modification strategies.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Note:} Research Question 3 is marked as optional due to the substantial scope of Questions 1 and 2. The theoretical development and implementation phases constitute the core contribution of this work, and the evaluation component will be pursued if time and complexity constraints permit.

\section{Planned Structure}

The thesis will be organized into seven chapters:

\begin{description}
    \item[Chapter 1: Introduction] Overview of the problem domain, motivation, and summary of contributions.
    \item[Chapter 2: Foundations] Background on classical planning formalisms, inconsistency measurement theory, and \ac{ASP}.
    \item[Chapter 3: Related Work] Survey of existing work on planning diagnostics and inconsistency measures.
    \item[Chapter 4: Theoretical Adaptation] Formal adaptation of selected inconsistency measures to planning, with definitions and examples.
    \item[Chapter 5: Implementation] \ac{ASP}-based algorithms and prototype development.
    \item[Chapter 6: Evaluation] Experimental setup, benchmarking results, and analysis.
    \item[Chapter 7: Conclusion] Summary of findings, limitations, and future work.
\end{description}
