\englishabstract{
    Classical planning problems become inconsistent when no valid action sequence can transform the initial state into a goal state. While inconsistency measurement has been well-developed in knowledge representation, its application to planning domains has received limited attention. This thesis aims to explore how established inconsistency measures can be adapted to classical planning problems in STRIPS and PDDL, with a focus on developing Answer Set Programming (ASP)-based algorithms for their computation. The work will investigate the practical utility of these measures for problem diagnosis and debugging. The goal is to provide planners with better tools for understanding why problems fail, potentially supporting more systematic approaches to constraint relaxation and problem reformulation.

\section{Introduction and Motivation}

Classical planning problems in artificial intelligence involve finding action sequences that transform an initial state into a goal state. When no valid action sequence exists, the problem is considered unsolvable. Most planning approaches focus on solvable instances, and when faced with unsolvable problems, they typically just report failure without much additional insight.

Inconsistency measurement is an established field in knowledge representation that quantifies the degree of inconsistency in logical knowledge bases. It provides ways to understand conflicts within sets of propositions and has been applied to various domains. However, its application to planning appears to be limited.

The motivation for this work comes from observing that many unsolvable planning problems seem to contain conflicting constraints or goals. For example, a problem might require an object to be in two different locations simultaneously, or actions might have effects that make certain goal combinations impossible. When planning systems encounter such conflicts, they usually just return "unsolvable" without indicating what went wrong or how the problem might be fixed.

It seems reasonable that quantifying the degree of inconsistency could help identify conflict sources and guide problem reformulation. This could be particularly useful when working with complex planning domains or automatically generated problems, where manually debugging failures can be tedious.

However, adapting inconsistency measures from knowledge representation to planning is not straightforward. Planning problems have a temporal structure and involve sequences of actions and state changes, which is significantly different from static logical knowledge bases. The types of conflicts that arise in planning may also be quite different from those in propositional logic. This means existing measures will need careful adaptation to capture the specific characteristics of planning problems.

\subsection{Inconsistency Measurement in Knowledge Representation}

Inconsistency measurement is a research area in knowledge representation that deals with quantifying the degree of inconsistency in logical knowledge bases. Several types of measures have been developed:

\textbf{Distance-based measures} calculate the minimal changes needed to make a knowledge base consistent. For example, the Max-Distance and Sum-Distance measures compute the Hamming distance between an inconsistent knowledge base and a nearest consistent version \cite{Kuhlmann2025}.

Recent work shows that ASP-based algorithms for computing inconsistency measures can outperform SAT-based approaches for many measures \cite{Kuhlmann2025}. This is relevant for planning applications, since ASP's declarative nature could be well-suited for modeling planning scenarios.

Additionally, Ulbricht et al. \cite{Ulbricht2016} developed inconsistency measures specifically for Answer Set Programs, demonstrating that classical monotonicity properties do not hold in non-monotonic logics. This insight could be useful when adapting measures to planning, where reasoning may involve non-monotonic aspects (for example, when using ASP to model the planning problem).

\subsection{Classical Planning}

Classical planning works with a state-based formalism where states are described by sets of facts, and actions are defined by preconditions and effects. The STRIPS (Stanford Research Institute Problem Solver) representation is foundational for many modern planning systems \cite{Russell2016}.

A planning problem becomes unsolvable when no sequence of applicable actions can transform the initial state into a goal state. The computational complexity of determining whether a plan exists is PSPACE-complete \cite{Russell2016}, which has motivated research into efficient heuristics and specialized algorithms.

\textbf{PDDL (Planning Domain Definition Language)} is a standard language for describing planning problems and is used in international planning competitions. Adapting inconsistency measures to work with PDDL representations will be important for practical applicability.

Modern planning systems use various search strategies and heuristics. However, when they encounter an unsolvable problem, they typically only report failure without providing analysis of why the problem is unsolvable or what could be changed to make it solvable.

\subsection{Planning and Inconsistency: Foundation Work}

Eriksson et al. \cite{Eriksson2018} established a theoretical connection between unsolvable planning problems and inconsistency. Their work proposes a proof system for demonstrating unsolvability in planning, examining why certain planning tasks cannot be solved using STRIPS-based approaches.

However, this work is primarily concerned with qualitative aspects of unsolvability rather than any quantitative measure of inconsistency. While Eriksson et al. provide important groundwork by formalizing how planning failures relate to logical inconsistency, the question of how to quantify different degrees of inconsistency in planning instances remains open.

Another line of research has approached unsolvable planning tasks from a diagnostic and explanatory perspective. Göbelbecker et al. \cite{Goebelbecker2010} introduced the concept of excuses for unsolvable planning tasks. An excuse is a minimal modification (such as removing or altering a specific goal or constraint) required to make an unsolvable problem solvable. This notion is analogous to identifying a minimal inconsistent subset in a logical knowledge base, providing a qualitative measure of how far a planning instance is from being solvable. More recently, Sreedharan et al. \cite{Sreedharan2019} developed methods to produce human-understandable explanations for unsolvable planning tasks by identifying unreachable subgoals. These efforts underscore the need for better diagnostic tools for planning failures, reinforcing the motivation to adapt inconsistency measurement techniques to this domain.

\section{Problem Statement and Research Objectives}

This thesis explores how inconsistency measures from knowledge representation can be adapted to classical planning and whether they provide practical value for understanding planning failures.

\subsection{Problem Statement}

When current planning systems encounter unsolvable problems, they provide very limited diagnostic information. This creates several challenges:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Users have little insight into why a given planning problem failed.
    \item There is no systematic way to analyze the structure of an unsolvable planning instance.
    \item It is difficult to identify what minimal changes could make the problem solvable (no support for problem reformulation).
    \item Different types of planning failures are not distinguished or quantified in any way.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Research Questions and Objectives}

The central research question of this thesis is:

\textbf{How can inconsistency measures from knowledge representation be effectively adapted to classical planning to provide practical diagnostic value for unsolvable planning problems?}

To address this question, the following objectives are defined:

\textbf{Theoretical Adaptation:} Determine how representative inconsistency measures can be transferred to classical planning. This involves developing a translation between planning problem representations and the frameworks used for inconsistency measurement, and examining which theoretical properties of the measures carry over to the planning context.

\textbf{Algorithmic Development:} Explore ASP-based algorithms for computing the adapt-ed measures in the context of planning. Prototype implementations will be developed that integrate with standard planning representations, and optimization techniques will be investigated to handle the combinatorial complexity.

\textbf{Empirical Evaluation:} Evaluate the adapted measures on benchmark planning problems (e.g., unsolvable instances from international planning competition domains). The evaluation will assess computational feasibility, scalability, and whether the measures provide interpretable information that helps differentiate and diagnose unsolvable ca-ses.

\textbf{Practical Applications:} Investigate how these inconsistency measures can support tools for planning problem diagnosis. This includes developing a proof-of-concept diagnostic tool or workflow that uses the measures to suggest potential problem relaxations or pinpoint conflicting constraints, and considering how the approach could integrate with existing planners or planning frameworks.

The thesis will be organized into eight chapters:

\begin{description}
    \item[Chapter 1: Introduction] Overview of the problem domain, motivation, and summary of contributions.
    \item[Chapter 2: Foundations] Background on classical planning formalisms, inconsistency measurement theory, and Answer Set Programming.

    \item[Chapter 4: Theoretical Framework] Formal adaptation of selected inconsistency measures to planning, with definitions, examples, and complexity analysis.
    \item[Chapter 5: Implementation] ASP-based algorithms, PDDL integration, and software architecture.
    \item[Chapter 6: Evaluation] Experimental setup, benchmark results, and final performance analysis.
    \item[Chapter 7: Applications and Case Studies] Diagnostic tool prototypes and illustrative examples.
    \item[Chapter 8: Conclusion] Summary of findings, limitations, and future work.
\end{description}
