\section{Introduction and Motivation}

In practical applications of automated planning, systems regularly encounter unsolvable problems. A planning instance is unsolvable if no sequence of available actions can transform the initial state into a goal state~\cite{Russell2016}. Since determining solvability is PSPACE-complete~\cite{Bylander1994}, unsolvable instances arise naturally in complex domains rather than being merely pathological cases. Consider a logistics scenario where a delivery company needs to route packages through a network of distribution centers. If the problem specifies that a package must be delivered to New York by noon while simultaneously requiring it to be processed at a facility in Los Angeles at the same time, the planning system will simply report "unsolvable" without explaining the conflicting requirements~\cite{Goebelbecker2010,Sreedharan2019}. Similarly, in robotics applications, a robot might be asked to manipulate objects in ways that violate physical constraints, or in manufacturing, production schedules might contain contradictory resource requirements~\cite{Goebelbecker2010}.

Beyond recognizing unsolvability, users require explanations of why the planner failed to find a solution. Current planning systems excel at finding solutions when they exist, but provide minimal diagnostic information when problems are unsolvable~\cite{Eriksson2018,Sreedharan2019}. This limitation becomes particularly problematic in complex domains where problems are automatically generated or when domain experts need to understand why their specifications cannot be satisfied~\cite{Sreedharan2019}. Manual debugging of such failures is impractical for large-scale problems with hundreds of constraints and actions, yet current approaches offer no systematic alternatives~\cite{Goebelbecker2010,Sreedharan2019}. The lack of quantitative diagnostic capabilities forces users into costly trial-and-error processes, significantly hindering the practical deployment of automated planning in real-world applications~\cite{Eriksson2018}.

The field of inconsistency measurement in knowledge representation and reasoning offers a promising approach to address this limitation~\cite{Grant2017,Thimm2016}. Rather than treating inconsistency as a binary property, these techniques quantify the degree of inconsistency within logical knowledge bases. Various measures have been developed to identify minimal inconsistent subsets, calculate distances to consistency, and count conflicting elements~\cite{Grant2011,Ulbricht2016}. However, these approaches have been primarily applied to static logical knowledge bases and have not been systematically adapted to the temporal and procedural nature of planning problems~\cite{Ulbricht2016,Corea2022,Condotta2024}.

While techniques for quantifying inconsistencies have been extensively developed in knowledge representation and reasoning, their systematic adaptation to planning domains remains largely unexplored. Planning problems present unique challenges for inconsistency measurement since, unlike propositional knowledge bases, planning involves sequences of actions that transform states over time~\cite{Russell2016}. Conflicts can arise not only from contradictory static constraints but also from action sequences that lead to impossible state combinations or from goals that cannot be reached given the available actions. The temporal structure means that inconsistencies might only emerge when considering specific action orderings or at particular time points in the planning horizon~\cite{Condotta2024}.

This research aims to bridge inconsistency measurement and planning by exploring how techniques from knowledge representation and reasoning can be adapted to quantify the degree of inconsistency in unsolvable planning instances. In this thesis, we develop quantitative diagnostics that characterize which goals or constraints drive a task's unsolvability. The approach focuses on \ac{ASP}-based algorithms, which are particularly well-suited for this task due to their declarative modeling capabilities, built-in support for temporal and combinatorial constraints, and recent performance improvements in solving optimization problems common in planning scenarios~\cite{Dimopoulos1997,DIMOPOULOS2019,Ulbricht2016,Kuhlmann2025}. If successful, such quantitative measures could provide systematic approaches to analyzing and fixing planning problems, filling a critical gap in current planning methodology.

\section{Technical Foundations and Related Work}

This section establishes the theoretical foundations by examining inconsistency measurement techniques, classical planning formalisms, and existing work connecting the domains.

\subsection{Inconsistency Measurement in Knowledge Representation and Reasoning}

Inconsistency measurement provides formal methods for quantifying the degree of inconsistency within logical knowledge bases. Rather than treating inconsistency as a binary property, these measures assign numerical values that reflect the severity or extent of conflicts, enabling comparative analysis and systematic approaches to conflict resolution~\cite{Thimm2019}.

Different approaches build on distinct core concepts~\cite{Grant2023}. Some measures focus on the structure of \ac{MIS}, counting or weighting the number of such subsets or analysing how formulas participate in conflicts. Examples include the MI-measure, its weighted variants, and the hitting-set measure. A hitting set is defined as a set of classical interpretations such that each formula of a knowledge base is satisfied by at least one interpretation, and the hitting-set measure looks for the smallest such set minus one~\cite{Thimm2019}.

Other approaches treat inconsistency as the distance to consistency. Using notions such as Dalal distance, they measure the minimal number of changes needed to obtain a consistent interpretation. The hit-distance measure, for instance, minimises the number of formulas whose models have a non-zero Dalal distance to a candidate interpretation~\cite{Grant2013}.

Another family of measures uses paraconsistent or many-valued logics to permit contradictory truth assignments. Priest's logic of paradox introduces a third truth value \emph{B} for "both true and false." The contension measure counts the minimal number of atoms that must be assigned this value to satisfy the knowledge base under such semantics~\cite{Thimm2019}. Recent work generalises this idea to broader families of three-valued or four-valued logics, emphasising that such measures can reveal conflicts inside formulas and thus avoid syntax sensitivity~\cite{Grant2023}.

Other approaches include forgetting-based, proof-based, and fuzzy-logic-based measures~\cite{Thimm2018}. For planning applications, computational efficiency and compatibility with non-monotonic reasoning are crucial. Experimental comparisons show that \ac{ASP}-based algorithms often outperform \ac{SAT}-based approaches for inconsistency measures~\cite{Kuhlmann2025}. The foundation for \ac{ASP}-based planning was established by~\cite{Dimopoulos1997}, with modern tools like plasp 3~\cite{DIMOPOULOS2019} providing efficient translations from \ac{PDDL} to \ac{ASP}. Importantly, inconsistency measures for \ac{ASP} have been developed~\cite{Ulbricht2016}, noting that the monotonicity postulate does not hold in non-monotonic logics—relevant for planning with its frame problem and default assumptions about action effects.

\subsection{Classical Planning}

Classical planning operates on a state-based formalism where the world is represented as a set of propositions that can be true or false in any given state. Actions are defined by their preconditions and effects: what must be true for the action to be applicable, and how the action changes the world state. The \ac{STRIPS} representation, originally developed by~\cite{Fikes1971}, is foundational for many modern planning systems~\cite{Russell2016}, providing a simple yet powerful framework for modeling planning domains where actions have deterministic effects and the world is fully observable.

A planning problem consists of three components: an initial state describing the starting configuration, a set of available actions, and a goal specification defining the desired outcome. The planning task is to find a sequence of actions that, when executed from the initial state, results in a state satisfying the goal conditions~\cite{Ghallab2004}.

Planning problems become unsolvable when no sequence of applicable actions can transform the initial state into a goal state. This can occur for several reasons: goals that are mutually exclusive and require contradictory states, required resources that are unavailable or insufficient, action preconditions that can never be satisfied, or action sequences that lead to dead-end states from which the goal cannot be reached. Determining whether a plan exists is PSPACE-complete~\cite{Bylander1994}, which has motivated extensive research into efficient algorithms and search techniques.

\ac{PDDL} serves as the standard language for describing planning problems and is used in international planning competitions~\cite{McDermott1998,Ghallab1998}. \ac{PDDL} provides a structured way to specify domains and problems, making it essential for practical applications. However, given the complexity of \ac{PDDL}'s various extensions and features, this thesis initially focuses on \ac{STRIPS}-based formulations due to their foundational simplicity and well-understood semantics, which allow for systematic adaptation of inconsistency measures without the complications of advanced \ac{PDDL} constructs.

Modern planning systems employ various search strategies to explore different ways of reaching goals, such as searching forward from the initial state, working backward from the goal, or examining the space of possible plans~\cite{Ghallab2004,Russell2016}. Important algorithmic advances include techniques that build graphical representations of planning problems to identify conflicts and estimate solution difficulty~\cite{Blum1997}, and smart search methods like \ac{HSP}~\cite{Bonet2001} and \ac{FF}~\cite{Hoffmann2001} that use simplified versions of problems to guide their search toward solutions. These heuristic approaches have significantly improved planning performance for solvable instances, but when these systems encounter an unsolvable problem, they typically provide minimal diagnostic information beyond reporting failure~\cite{Eriksson2018}. This diagnostic gap represents a significant limitation for users attempting to understand the source of conflicts or determine appropriate modifications to achieve solvability.

\subsection{Inconsistency and Planning: Foundation Work}

The connection between inconsistency and planning has been explored from several perspectives, though quantitative inconsistency measurement remains largely unexplored in the planning literature. While the broader field of inconsistency measurement in knowledge representation has developed sophisticated techniques for quantifying conflicts in logical knowledge bases~\cite{Thimm2019,Grant2017,Grant2023}, these approaches have not been systematically adapted to address the unique challenges posed by planning problems' temporal and procedural nature.

Important theoretical groundwork connecting unsolvable planning problems to logical inconsistency has been established by~\cite{Eriksson2018}. This work proposes a proof system for demonstrating unsolvability in planning, examining why certain planning tasks cannot be solved using \ac{STRIPS}-based approaches. While this research formalizes how planning failures relate to logical inconsistency, it focuses on proving unsolvability rather than measuring degrees of inconsistency.

From a diagnostic perspective, the concept of "excuses" for unsolvable planning tasks has been introduced by~\cite{Goebelbecker2010}. An excuse represents a minimal modification required to make an unsolvable problem solvable, such as removing or altering a specific goal or constraint. This approach is conceptually similar to identifying \ac{MIS} in logical knowledge bases~\cite{Thimm2019}. However, excuses provide only binary guidance about what to remove rather than quantitative measures of inconsistency severity, and lack the comparative analysis capabilities that would allow users to understand the relative complexity of different conflicts or prioritize repair strategies.

Complementing these diagnostic approaches, methods for producing explanations that help users understand why planning tasks are unsolvable have been developed by~\cite{Sreedharan2019}, focusing on the identification of unreachable subgoals. This approach emphasizes explanatory aspects rather than quantitative measurement, but demonstrates the practical value of systematic analysis when planning problems are unsolvable. While valuable for understanding individual cases of unsolvability, these explanation methods do not provide the quantitative measures needed for systematic problem analysis, automated repair, or comparative evaluation of different unsolvable instances.

These research efforts highlight the need for better diagnostic tools while revealing current limitations. Unlike static knowledge bases where inconsistency measurement has been extensively studied~\cite{Thimm2016,Thimm2019,Grant2017}, planning problems involve temporal sequences and procedural constraints that create new conflict types. While existing work provides valuable insights into individual aspects of unsolvable planning problems, there remains a clear gap between binary diagnostic approaches and the quantitative analysis capabilities needed for systematic problem understanding and repair. This thesis addresses this gap by adapting inconsistency measurement techniques to planning, providing quantitative measures that can systematically compare inconsistency degrees across different planning instances.

\section{Problem Statement}

Current planning systems provide minimal diagnostic information when encountering unsolvable problems. Users lack insight into why problems are unsolvable and have no systematic way to analyze the structure of unsolvable instances. This makes it difficult to identify minimal changes that could restore solvability, as different types of unsolvable planning problems are not distinguished or quantified. The lack of quantitative diagnostic capabilities forces users into trial-and-error processes, significantly limiting the practical applicability of automated planning in complex real-world scenarios.

\section{Research Questions and Objectives}

The central research question of this thesis is:

\begin{quote}
    How can inconsistency measures from knowledge representation and reasoning be meaningfully adapted to classical planning to provide diagnostic value for unsolvable planning problems?
\end{quote}

This central question is addressed through the following research questions and their corresponding objectives:

\subsection{Research Question 1: Theoretical Adaptation}

\begin{quote}
    Which inconsistency measures from knowledge representation and reasoning can be adapted to classical planning, and what postulates do they retain?
\end{quote}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item[(1a)] Survey and categorize existing inconsistency measures from knowledge representation and reasoning.
    \item[(1b)] Analyze the structural differences between knowledge bases and planning problems, identifying adaptation challenges.
    \item[(1c)] Develop formal adaptations of 2-3 selected inconsistency measures to \ac{STRIPS}-based planning formulations.
    \item[(1d)] Examine which postulates are preserved or modified in the planning context.
    \item[(1e)] Provide formal definitions and examples of the adapted measures.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Research Question 2: Computational Implementation}

\begin{quote}
    How can the adapted measures be efficiently computed for planning problems using Answer Set Programming?
\end{quote}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item[(2a)] Design \ac{ASP}-based encodings for the adapted inconsistency measures.
    \item[(2b)] Develop algorithms that handle standard planning problem formats, including \ac{STRIPS} and optionally basic \ac{PDDL} representations.
    \item[(2c)] Implement a prototype system that can parse planning problems and compute the adapted measures.
    \item[(2d)] Analyze computational complexity and if possible optimize \ac{ASP} encodings for scalability in planning domains.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Research Question 3: Practical Evaluation (Optional)}

\begin{quote}
    Do the adapted measures provide practical diagnostic value for understanding and analyzing unsolvable planning problems?
\end{quote}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item[(3a)] Establish an experimental framework using benchmark planning problems from established collections.
    \item[(3b)] Evaluate computational feasibility and scalability of the implemented measures on problems of varying complexity.
    \item[(3c)] Analyze the diagnostic utility of the measures for distinguishing different types of unsolvable planning problems.
    \item[(3d)] Assess whether the measures provide actionable insights for problem repair and modification strategies.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Note:} Research Question 3 is marked as optional due to the substantial scope of Questions 1 and 2. The theoretical development and implementation phases constitute the core contribution of this work, and the evaluation component will be pursued if time and complexity constraints permit.

The thesis will be organized into seven chapters:

\begin{description}
    \item[Chapter 1: Introduction] Overview of the problem domain, motivation, and summary of contributions.
    \item[Chapter 2: Foundations] Background on classical planning formalisms, inconsistency measurement theory, and \ac{ASP}.
    \item[Chapter 3: Related Work] Survey of existing work on planning diagnostics and inconsistency measures.
    \item[Chapter 4: Theoretical Adaptation] Formal adaptation of selected inconsistency measures to planning, with definitions and examples.
    \item[Chapter 5: Implementation] \ac{ASP}-based algorithms and prototype development.
    \item[Chapter 6: Evaluation] Experimental setup, benchmarking results, and analysis.
    \item[Chapter 7: Conclusion] Summary of findings, limitations, and future work.
\end{description}
