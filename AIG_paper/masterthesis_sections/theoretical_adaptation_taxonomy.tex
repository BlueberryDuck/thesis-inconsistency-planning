\subsection{Structural Taxonomy for Unsolvable Planning Problems}
\label{sec:taxonomy}

Before presenting the proposed measures, this section establishes a structural taxonomy that organizes unsolvable planning problems by features relevant to inconsistency measurement. This taxonomy identifies distinct measurement targets and motivates the design choices in subsequent sections.

Unsolvable planning problems can be organized by structural features that suggest different approaches for inconsistency measurement. This taxonomy is organized by mathematical ideas needed to detect and quantify conflicts, rather than by mutually exclusive categories. The three structural categories are:

\textbf{Category 1: Goal Specification Conflicts.} The goal specification $G$ contains propositions that cannot simultaneously be true in any valid state. In classical propositional logic, this would include explicit contradictions such as $\{p, \neg p\}$. However, in propositional \ac{PDDL} with \texttt{:strips} requirements, goal specifications consist exclusively of positive literals; negative goal literals require \ac{PDDL} 3.0 extensions~\cite{Gerevini2005}. Consequently, specification-level contradictions of the form $\{p, \neg p\}$ cannot occur in the planning formalism addressed by this thesis.

Nevertheless, goal propositions may be \emph{operator-induced mutex} (mutual exclusion): each proposition is individually achievable, but action effects structurally prevent their simultaneous satisfaction in any reachable state. For instance, a goal requiring both $\{\textit{light\_on}, \textit{light\_off}\}$ when operators enforce mutual exclusion, where turning on deletes off and vice versa, cannot be satisfied by any reachable state. This operator-induced mutex represents the planning analog of specification-level conflicts. This category is detectable through reachability analysis of $G$ against the state space induced by $O$, and suggests logical and combinatorial measures such as counting mutex pairs, identifying maximal conflicting sets (largest subsets of $G$ where all pairs are mutex), or computing minimal covering sets (smallest subsets whose removal eliminates all mutex relationships).

\textbf{Category 2: Dependency Graph Structure.} Problems arise in the precondition and effect relationships between propositions and operators. The \emph{precondition-effect dependency graph} captures these relationships: nodes represent propositions, and directed edges connect preconditions to effects through operators, indicating that achieving a proposition $p$ (as an effect) requires first satisfying proposition $q$ (as a precondition). For example, if operator $o$ has $\text{precond}(o) = \{q\}$ and $\text{add}(o) = \{p\}$, the graph contains an edge from $q$ to $p$ labeled with $o$.

This category encompasses three structural features analyzable via graph algorithms on the dependency graph:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{(2a) Missing producers:} No operator produces a required proposition. In the dependency graph, a goal proposition has no incoming edges. For example, unlocking a door when no operator can produce the required key.
    \item \textbf{(2b) Circular dependencies:} Preconditions form cycles without initialization. In the dependency graph, a cycle exists where no node in the cycle is initially true. For example, requiring a security badge to be activated to enter a building while needing to be inside the building to activate the badge.
    \item \textbf{(2c) Action-based exclusions:} Achieving certain goal propositions via irreversible actions creates dead-end states preventing other goal propositions from being reached. For example, entering a one-way door to retrieve an item while becoming trapped and unable to complete other objectives.
\end{itemize}
This category suggests graph-based measures such as counting missing producers, measuring precondition chain depth, detecting cycles, or analyzing reachability from hypothetical goal states.

\textbf{Category 3: Resource Constraints.} Consumable resources (such as time, fuel, budget, or battery) are insufficient to satisfy goal requirements. This includes both total resource deficit, where the cumulative consumption exceeds the available supply (such as requiring two fuel-consuming moves but having only one unit of fuel), and resource contention, where multiple goal propositions compete for the same limited resource (such as two surfaces requiring painting but only one can of paint being available). This category requires numeric tracking and accounting measures, such as computing absolute or relative resource deficits, counting over-subscribed resources, or tracking path-dependent consumption patterns.

These structural categories are not mutually exclusive and a single planning problem may exhibit multiple categories simultaneously. For instance, resource contention problems (Category 3) often exhibit goal proposition conflicts (Category 1) caused by resource consumption. The taxonomy is organized by what measurement approaches are applicable: logical analysis for goal proposition conflicts, graph algorithms for dependency structures, and numeric accounting for resources.

\subsubsection{Scope of This Thesis}

This work develops inconsistency measures for operator-induced goal conflicts (the planning analog of Category~1) and selected aspects of Category~2, focusing on conflicts detectable through reachability-based analysis of propositional \ac{PDDL} planning problems. Specifically, three measures are proposed: one for operator-induced mutex relationships where goals are individually achievable but never simultaneously satisfiable (Goal Mutex Profile), one for irreversible action-based exclusions where achieving certain goals permanently blocks others (Goal Sequencing Conflict Profile), and one providing generic unreachability diagnosis applicable across Category~2 subtypes (Unreachability Profile).

Category 2b (circular dependencies) and Category 3 (resource constraints) are recognized as important sources of unsolvability but are excluded from formal measurement in this thesis. Circular dependencies cause unreachability symptoms that are captured by the generic unreachability measure; however, providing specific diagnostics for cycles would require graph-theoretic analysis beyond reachability computation. While such analysis could yield more targeted repair suggestions (identifying which dependency edge to break versus which producer to add), this thesis prioritizes breadth of coverage over depth of cause-specific diagnosis. The generic unreachability measure identifies which propositions are blocked; distinguishing why they are blocked (missing producer versus cycle versus action-based exclusion) is recognized as valuable future work but is orthogonal to the current goal-level and dependency-level framework.

Resource constraints (Category 3) are excluded for two reasons: first, from a knowledge representation perspective, numeric resource insufficiency often reduces to missing producer problems (Category 2a); second, numeric fluents introduce significant complexity through \ac{PDDL} 2.1 extensions~\cite{Fox2003}, requiring fundamentally different measurement approaches. This thesis focuses on propositional \ac{PDDL} planning with the \texttt{:strips} and \texttt{:typing} requirements; numeric extensions warrant separate treatment. Future work may develop specialized measures for cycles and numeric resources, building on the foundational framework established here.

An important practical distinction emerges between static and dynamic analysis approaches. Static issues can be detected directly from the problem specification $\langle P, O, I, G \rangle$ without state space exploration: goal proposition conflicts are identified by checking $G$ for mutex propositions, missing producers by verifying whether $\exists o \in O$ with $p \in \text{add}(o)$, and simple resource bounds by comparing initial quantities against total requirements. Dynamic issues, conversely, require reasoning about reachable states or execution paths: circular dependencies necessitate graph analysis of precondition chains, unreachable regions require forward or backward reachability computation, and path-dependent resource consumption demands state transition tracking. This static-dynamic distinction has implications for computational complexity and suggests that measures based on static analysis may be more efficiently computable than those requiring dynamic state space reasoning.

\begin{example}[Multiple Structural Categories]
    \label{ex:security_facility}
    To illustrate how multiple structural categories can manifest in a single planning problem, consider a security facility scenario:
    \begin{align*}
        P & = \{\textit{outside}, \textit{inside}, \textit{has\_badge}, \textit{vault\_accessed}, \textit{lights\_on}, \textit{lights\_off}\}   \\
        O & \ni \textit{enter\_facility} = \langle \{\textit{outside}, \textit{has\_badge}\}, \{\textit{inside}\}, \{\textit{outside}\} \rangle \\
          & \ni \textit{access\_vault} = \langle \{\textit{inside}\}, \{\textit{vault\_accessed}\}, \{\} \rangle                                \\
          & \ni \textit{turn\_on} = \langle \{\textit{lights\_off}\}, \{\textit{lights\_on}\}, \{\textit{lights\_off}\} \rangle                 \\
          & \ni \textit{turn\_off} = \langle \{\textit{lights\_on}\}, \{\textit{lights\_off}\}, \{\textit{lights\_on}\} \rangle                 \\
        I & = \{\textit{outside}, \textit{lights\_off}\}                                                                                        \\
        G & = \{\textit{vault\_accessed}, \textit{outside}, \textit{lights\_on}, \textit{lights\_off}\}
    \end{align*}

    This problem exhibits three distinct structural sources of unsolvability. First, Category~1 (operator-induced mutex): the goal requires both \textit{lights\_on} and \textit{lights\_off}, but these propositions are mutually exclusive through operator effects, as achieving one necessarily deletes the other. Second, Category~2a (missing producer): the proposition \textit{has\_badge} appears in the precondition of \textit{enter\_facility} but no operator in $O$ has \textit{has\_badge} in its add effects, making facility entry impossible regardless of other constraints. Third, Category~2c (action-based exclusion): even if a badge-producing operator existed, \textit{enter\_facility} deletes \textit{outside} without any operator to restore it, while the goal requires both \textit{vault\_accessed} (achievable only from inside) and \textit{outside}, creating an irreversible conflict. Each category contributes independently to unsolvability, demonstrating how the taxonomy identifies structurally distinct measurement targets within a single problem.
\end{example}
