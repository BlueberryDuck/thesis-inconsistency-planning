\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

In practical applications of automated planning, systems regularly encounter unsolvable problems. A planning instance is unsolvable if no sequence of available actions can transform the initial state into a goal state~\cite{Russell2016}. Since determining solvability is PSPACE-complete~\cite{Bylander1994}, unsolvable instances arise naturally in complex domains rather than being merely edge cases. Consider a logistics scenario where a delivery company needs to route packages through a network of distribution centers. If the problem specifies that a package must be delivered to New York by noon while simultaneously requiring it to be processed at a facility in Los Angeles at the same time, the planning system will report ``unsolvable'' without explaining the conflicting requirements~\cite{Goebelbecker2010,Sreedharan2019}. Similarly, in robotics applications, a robot might be asked to manipulate objects in ways that violate physical constraints, or in manufacturing, production schedules might contain contradictory resource requirements.

Beyond recognizing unsolvability, users require explanations of why the planner failed to find a solution. Current planning systems excel at finding solutions when they exist, but provide minimal diagnostic information when problems are unsolvable~\cite{Eriksson2018,Sreedharan2019}. This limitation becomes particularly problematic in complex domains where problems are automatically generated or when domain experts need to understand why their specifications cannot be satisfied~\cite{Sreedharan2019}. Manual debugging of such failures is impractical for large-scale problems with hundreds of constraints and actions, yet current approaches offer no systematic alternatives~\cite{Goebelbecker2010,Sreedharan2019}. The lack of quantitative diagnostic capabilities forces users into costly trial-and-error processes, significantly hindering the practical deployment of automated planning in real-world applications~\cite{Eriksson2018}.

The field of inconsistency measurement in propositional logic~\cite{Thimm2019} offers a promising approach to address this limitation. Rather than treating inconsistency as a binary property, these techniques quantify the degree of inconsistency within logical knowledge bases. A multitude of measures have been introduced, each capturing different aspects of logical conflicts. Minimal inconsistent and maximal consistent set-based measures~\cite{Hunter2008}, distance-based measures~\cite{Grant2013,Grant2017}, a hitting-set measure~\cite{Thimm2016}, a contension-based measure~\cite{Grant2011,Thimm2016}, and forgetting-based measures~\cite{Besnard2016} have all been developed to provide formal methods for quantifying the severity of inconsistencies. These measures enable comparative analysis and systematic approaches to conflict resolution, allowing users to identify which parts of a knowledge base contribute most significantly to its inconsistency~\cite{Thimm2019}.

However, while these approaches have been primarily applied to logical knowledge bases and have recently been adapted to other domains such as declarative process specifications~\cite{Corea2022} and temporal reasoning~\cite{Condotta2024}, their systematic adaptation to planning domains remains largely unexplored. Planning problems present unique challenges for inconsistency measurement since, unlike propositional knowledge bases, planning involves sequences of actions that transform states over time~\cite{Russell2016}. Conflicts can arise not only from contradictory static constraints but also from action sequences that lead to impossible state combinations or from goals that cannot be reached given the available actions.

This research aims to bridge inconsistency measurement and planning by exploring how techniques from propositional logic can be adapted to quantify the degree of inconsistency in unsolvable planning instances. The thesis develops quantitative diagnostics that characterize which goals or constraints drive a task's unsolvability. The approach focuses on \ac{ASP}-based algorithms~\cite{Ulbricht2016}, which are well-suited for this task due to their declarative modeling capabilities and support for temporal and combinatorial constraints~\cite{Dimopoulos1997,Dimopoulos2019}. Recent work demonstrates that \ac{ASP}-based approaches generally outperform \ac{SAT}-based methods for computing inconsistency measures~\cite{Kuhlmann2025}, supporting the choice of \ac{ASP} for this domain adaptation. If successful, such quantitative measures could provide systematic approaches to analyzing and fixing planning problems, filling a critical gap in current planning methodology.

\section{Problem Statement}

Current planning systems provide minimal diagnostic information when encountering unsolvable problems. Users lack insight into why problems are unsolvable and have no systematic way to analyze the structure of unsolvable instances. This makes it difficult to identify minimal changes that could restore solvability, as different types of unsolvable planning problems are not distinguished or quantified. The lack of quantitative diagnostic capabilities forces users into trial-and-error processes, significantly limiting the practical applicability of automated planning in complex real-world scenarios.

\section{Research Questions and Objectives}

The central research question of this thesis is:

\begin{quote}
    How can inconsistency measures from propositional logic be meaningfully adapted to classical planning to provide diagnostic value for unsolvable planning problems?
\end{quote}

This central question is addressed through the following research questions and their corresponding objectives:

\subsection{Research Question 1: Theoretical Adaptation}

\begin{quote}
    Which inconsistency measures from propositional logic can be adapted to classical planning, and what postulates do they retain?
\end{quote}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item[(1a)] Survey existing inconsistency measures from propositional logic.
    \item[(1b)] Analyze the structural differences between knowledge bases and planning problems, identifying adaptation challenges.
    \item[(1c)] Develop formal adaptations of 2-3 selected inconsistency measures to \ac{STRIPS}-based planning formulations.
    \item[(1d)] Examine which postulates are preserved or modified in the planning context.
    \item[(1e)] Provide formal definitions and examples of the adapted measures.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Research Question 2: Computational Implementation}

\begin{quote}
    How can the adapted measures be efficiently computed for planning problems using Answer Set Programming?
\end{quote}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item[(2a)] Analyze computational complexity of the adapted measures to ensure compatibility with \ac{ASP}.
    \item[(2b)] Design an \ac{ASP}-based encoding for at least one adapted inconsistency measure.
    \item[(2c)] Develop parsing algorithms that handle standard planning problem formats, including \ac{STRIPS} and optionally basic \ac{PDDL} representations.
    \item[(2d)] Implement a prototype system for at least one measure that can parse planning problems and compute the adapted measures.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Research Question 3: Practical Evaluation}

\begin{quote}
    Do the adapted measures provide practical diagnostic value for understanding and analyzing unsolvable planning problems?
\end{quote}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item[(3a)] Establish an experimental framework using benchmark planning problems from established collections.
    \item[(3b)] Evaluate computational feasibility and scalability of at least one implemented measure on problems of varying complexity.
    \item[(3c)] Analyze the diagnostic utility of the implemented measure(s) for distinguishing different types of unsolvable planning problems.
    \item[(3d)] Assess whether the measures provide actionable insights for problem repair and modification strategies (optional).
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Note:} To maintain feasibility within the thesis scope, the implementation and evaluation will focus on at least one measure, with extensions to additional measures marked as optional.
