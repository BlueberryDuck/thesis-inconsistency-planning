\subsection{Inconsistency Measurement}

Inconsistency measurement provides formal methods for quantifying the degree of inconsistency within logical knowledge bases. Rather than treating inconsistency as a binary property, these measures assign numerical values that reflect the severity or extent of conflicts, enabling comparative analysis and systematic approaches to conflict resolution~\cite{Thimm2019}.

\subsubsection{Knowledge Bases and Inconsistency Measures}

\begin{defn}[Knowledge Base~\cite{Thimm2019}]
    \label{defn:knowledge_base}
    A \emph{knowledge base} $K$ is a finite set of propositional formulas. Let $\mathbb{K}$ denote the set of all finite propositional knowledge bases. A knowledge base $K$ is \emph{consistent} if there exists a truth assignment satisfying all formulas in $K$; otherwise it is \emph{inconsistent}.
\end{defn}

\begin{example}[Consistent and Inconsistent Knowledge Bases]
    The knowledge base $K_1 = \{a, a \rightarrow b\}$ is consistent: the truth assignment $a = b = \text{true}$ satisfies both formulas. The knowledge base $K_2 = \{a, \neg a\}$ is inconsistent since no truth assignment can satisfy both formulas simultaneously.
\end{example}

An inconsistency measure provides a systematic way to assign numerical values reflecting the degree of inconsistency.

\begin{defn}[Inconsistency Measure~\cite{Thimm2019}]
    \label{defn:inconsistency_measure}
    An \emph{inconsistency measure} is a function $I : \mathbb{K} \to \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}$ that maps each knowledge base to a non-negative real number quantifying its degree of inconsistency.
\end{defn}

Inconsistency measures are typically required to satisfy certain rationality postulates that capture intuitive properties of inconsistency quantification. Hunter and Konieczny~\cite{Hunter2008} and Thimm~\cite{Thimm2019} identify the following core postulates:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Consistency:} $I(K) = 0$ if and only if $K$ is consistent. This ensures the measure correctly identifies the absence of conflicts.
    \item \textbf{Monotonicity:} If $K \subseteq K'$, then $I(K) \leq I(K')$. Adding formulas cannot decrease inconsistency in classical propositional logic.
    \item \textbf{Free Formula Independence:} $I(K \cup \{\phi\}) = I(K)$ if $\phi$ is not involved in any inconsistency of $K \cup \{\phi\}$. Irrelevant formulas do not affect the measure.
\end{enumerate}

\begin{example}[Rationality Postulates]
    Consider $K = \{a, \neg a\}$. By Consistency, $I(K) > 0$ since $K$ is inconsistent. Adding the formula $b$ yields $K' = \{a, \neg a, b\}$; by Monotonicity, $I(K') \geq I(K)$. Since $b$ shares no atoms with the conflict and is therefore not involved in any inconsistency, Free Formula Independence implies $I(K') = I(K)$.
\end{example}

These postulates may require modification when adapting measures to specific domains. In particular, Monotonicity assumes that adding information can only increase inconsistency, which does not hold in non-monotonic contexts where new information can resolve conflicts.

\subsubsection{The Contension Measure}

Several approaches to inconsistency measurement exist, including syntactic approaches based on minimal inconsistent subsets and semantic approaches based on model-theoretic analysis~\cite{Thimm2019}. Among semantic approaches, the contension measure $I_c$ introduced by Grant and Hunter~\cite{Grant2011} is particularly relevant as a baseline for domain adaptations.

The contension measure employs three-valued paraconsistent semantics based on Priest's Logic of Paradox~\cite{Priest1979}, where propositions can be assigned a third truth value $B$ (``both true and false'') representing conflict. The measure counts the minimum number of \emph{atoms} that must be assigned $B$ to satisfy the knowledge base under three-valued semantics:
\begin{equation*}
    I_c(K) = \min\{|\upsilon^{-1}(B) \cap \text{At}(K)| \mid \upsilon \models_3 K\}
\end{equation*}
where $\text{At}(K)$ denotes the atoms appearing in $K$ and $\upsilon \models_3 K$ indicates that $\upsilon$ satisfies $K$ under three-valued semantics.

\begin{example}[Contension Measure]
    Consider $K = \{a \land b, \neg a, \neg b\}$. Setting both $a$ and $b$ to $B$ satisfies all formulas: $a \land b$ evaluates to $B$ (the minimum of $B$ and $B$), and $\neg a$ and $\neg b$ both evaluate to $B$ (negation of $B$ is $B$). Since the truth value $B$ propagates through negation, this is the minimal resolution, yielding $I_c(K) = 2$.
\end{example}

The contension measure counts atoms, not formulas or conflicts. This means that multiple formula conflicts may be resolved by assigning $B$ to a single shared atom. For instance, the knowledge base $K = \{a \land b, \neg a \land b, \neg a \land c, a \land c\}$ contains multiple pairwise conflicts but can be satisfied by setting only $a$ to $B$, yielding $I_c(K) = 1$. This atom-level granularity distinguishes contension from formula-counting measures.

Section~\ref{sec:naive_encoding} demonstrates that despite this granularity, $I_c$ yields uninformative results when applied to naive propositional encodings of planning problems.

\subsubsection{Domain Adaptation Precedents}

Inconsistency measurement techniques have been successfully adapted to diverse logical domains, establishing methodological precedent for this thesis.

Ulbricht et al.~\cite{Ulbricht2016} adapted inconsistency measures to \ac{ASP}, addressing the fundamental challenge that non-monotonic reasoning violates classical monotonicity postulates. Their key observation, that adding information to a non-monotonic knowledge base can reduce inconsistency, directly parallels the planning context, where adding operators can enable new solution paths. This motivates the Operator Monotonicity postulate developed in Section~\ref{sec:postulates}.

Corea et al.~\cite{Corea2022} developed inconsistency measures for temporal logic applied to declarative process specifications. Their work demonstrates that traditional measures fail to adequately capture temporal operators, requiring domain-specific semantics for meaningful conflict quantification. This parallels the finding in this thesis that naive propositional encodings of planning problems yield uninformative measure values, motivating planning-native measures.

These adaptations establish that inconsistency measures can be meaningfully extended to diverse formalisms when domain-specific conflict semantics are carefully considered.
