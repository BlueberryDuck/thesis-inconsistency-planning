\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related_work}

The connection between inconsistency and planning has been explored from several perspectives, with foundational work establishing important theoretical and diagnostic approaches for unsolvable planning problems. While the broader field of inconsistency measurement in propositional logic has developed various techniques for quantifying conflicts in logical knowledge bases~\cite{Thimm2019,Grant2023}, their systematic adaptation to planning domains remains an open research direction.

Important theoretical groundwork on proof systems for demonstrating unsolvability in planning has been established by~\cite{Eriksson2018}. This work introduces the concept of ``dead states'' (states from which no goal state is reachable) and proposes a rule-based proof system for certifying unsolvability. This concept connects directly to our Goal Sequencing Conflict Profile: a sequencing conflict $(g_1, g_2)$ exists precisely when all reachable states satisfying $g_1$ are dead with respect to achieving $g_2$. While~\cite{Eriksson2018} provides binary certificates (solvable/unsolvable), our measure quantifies the extent of such conflicts, revealing how many goal pairs exhibit sequencing conflicts and whether conflicts are unidirectional or symmetric.

From a diagnostic perspective, \cite{Goebelbecker2010} introduced ``excuses'' for unsolvable planning tasks: minimal modifications required to restore solvability, such as adding initial facts, introducing operators, or weakening goals. While excuses provide actionable repair suggestions, our measures complement this by providing quantitative structural information without prescribing specific repairs. The Unreachability Profile identifies which propositions cannot be achieved, suggesting where ``add producer'' excuses are needed. The Goal Sequencing Conflict Profile identifies irreversible action sequences, suggesting where ``add reversal operator'' excuses apply. Following the inconsistency measurement principle of ``measuring what IS, not what SHOULD BE,'' we report objective structural properties, leaving repair decisions to domain experts.

The planning literature has also extensively studied mutex (mutual exclusion) relationships, particularly in the context of Graphplan~\cite{Blum1997}. Classical mutex analysis identifies proposition pairs that cannot be simultaneously true at a given planning level, used heuristically during search. Our Goal Mutex Profile extends this concept to goal-level diagnostics: we identify goals that are each individually achievable but never co-occur in any reachable state, quantifying both scope (number of involved goals) and structure (number of mutex pairs).

Complementing these approaches, methods for producing explanations that help users understand why planning tasks are unsolvable have been developed by~\cite{Sreedharan2019}, focusing on the identification of unreachable subgoals. While valuable for understanding individual cases, these explanation methods do not provide the quantitative measures needed for systematic comparison across different unsolvable instances.

These research efforts establish important foundations for understanding unsolvable planning problems. However, they share a common limitation: existing approaches primarily focus on binary characterizations or prescriptive repairs rather than quantitative structural analysis. This thesis bridges the gap by adapting inconsistency measurement methodology (formal definitions, rationality postulates, complexity analysis) to provide systematic quantitative diagnostics for planning problems.
