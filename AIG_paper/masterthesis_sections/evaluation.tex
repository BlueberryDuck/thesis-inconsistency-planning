\section{Evaluation}
\label{sec:evaluation}

This chapter evaluates the implemented measures on unsolvable planning benchmarks from the IPC~2016 Unsolvability Competition. The evaluation addresses Research Question~3: whether the adapted measures provide practical diagnostic value for understanding and analyzing unsolvable planning problems. Section~\ref{sec:eval_methodology} describes the experimental setup and dataset. Section~\ref{sec:eval_results} presents the benchmark results across six compatible domains. Section~\ref{sec:eval_case_studies} examines individual problems that illustrate the diagnostic capabilities and limitations of the measures. Section~\ref{sec:eval_horizon} analyzes the sensitivity of results to the horizon parameter, revealing a precision-recall tradeoff that constitutes a key methodological finding. Section~\ref{sec:eval_performance} discusses computational performance and scalability. Section~\ref{sec:eval_discussion} synthesizes the findings and addresses limitations.

\subsection{Evaluation Methodology}
\label{sec:eval_methodology}

\subsubsection{Dataset}

The evaluation uses two benchmark suites. The primary dataset is the IPC~2016 Unsolvability Competition benchmark suite by Muise and Lipovetzky~\cite{Muise2016}, which contains 15 domains of unsolvable planning problems specifically designed for evaluating unsolvability detection techniques. Each domain includes between 15 and 25 unsolvable problem instances of increasing size, and several domains also include solvable instances (\texttt{satprob} files) for validation purposes. The secondary dataset is the unsolvable benchmark collection by Eriksson~\cite{Eriksson2019}, which extends the IPC~2016 domains and adds new domains including \textit{3unsat}, a set of unsatisfiable 3-SAT instances encoded as planning problems.

Of the 15 IPC~2016 domains, 6 are compatible with the implementation pipeline. One additional domain from the Eriksson benchmarks, \textit{3unsat}, is also compatible. Table~\ref{tab:domain_compatibility} summarizes the compatibility status. Two domains (\textit{bag-barman} and \textit{tetris}) use the \texttt{:equality} requirement, which \textit{plasp} does not support, as noted in Section~\ref{sec:pddl_translation}. Seven domains produce grounding programs that exceed the 120-second timeout: \textit{bag-gripper}, \textit{bag-transport}, \textit{over-nomystery}, \textit{over-rovers}, \textit{over-tpp}, \textit{sliding-tiles}, and \textit{pegsol}. The compatible domains span a range of unsolvability types: \textit{diagnosis} exhibits unreachability, mutex, and sequencing conflicts; \textit{bottleneck} and \textit{chessboard-pebbling} exhibit pure mutex and sequencing conflicts without unreachability; \textit{cave-diving}, \textit{document-transfer}, and \textit{pegsol-row5} primarily exhibit unreachability; and \textit{3unsat} encodes combinatorial unsatisfiability outside the scope of the proposed measures.

\subsubsection{Experimental Setup}

All experiments run inside a Docker container with Python~3.12, Clingo~5.8.0, and \textit{plasp}~3.1.1. The horizon parameter is set to $h = 20$ for all runs, and each problem has a timeout of 120 seconds. The batch runner (Section~\ref{sec:tooling}) processes all domain/problem pairs in each compatible domain, recording the full diagnostic profile, category classification, problem size metadata (goal, proposition, and operator counts), computation time, and status (OK, TIMEOUT, or ERROR) for each instance.

\subsubsection{Other Domains}

\paragraph{Bottleneck.} Twelve of 25 instances complete, all exhibiting zero unreachability ($\mathcal{I}^{\text{scope}}_{\text{UR}} = 0$). Ten are classified as 2c-sequencing and two as consistent. The conflict counts decrease as the grid grows: $\mathcal{I}^{\text{scope}}_{\text{MX}}$ drops from 4 (prob01, 4 goals) to 2 (prob10, 6 goals), before reaching 0 in prob11 and prob12. This pattern reflects the interaction between grid size and horizon: larger grids provide more delete-relaxed paths for goals to coexist, resolving apparent conflicts. Section~\ref{sec:eval_horizon} confirms that the two consistent instances exhibit conflicts at a shorter horizon.

\paragraph{Cave-diving.} Eight of 25 unsolvable instances complete, of which seven are classified as consistent and one (prob05) as Category~2a with a single unreachable goal. The dominance of consistent classifications is striking for a domain of unsolvable problems. Section~\ref{sec:eval_horizon} demonstrates that these classifications are horizon-dependent: all seven instances exhibit sequencing conflicts at $h = 10$.

\paragraph{Document-transfer.} Only 3 of 20 unsolvable instances complete due to large grounding sizes. All three are classified as Category~2a with heavy unreachability: prob01 and prob03 have 19 out of 19 goals unreachable, indicating that no goal proposition is reachable in these instances.

\paragraph{Pegsol-row5.} Three of 15 instances complete, all classified as Category~2a with exactly one unreachable goal. The domain exhibits extreme exponential growth in problem size: prob01 has 228 propositions and 216 operators, prob02 has 2{,}772 and 2{,}744, and prob03 has 13{,}872 and 13{,}824, creating a sharp computational wall after prob03.

\paragraph{Chessboard-pebbling.} Three of 23 instances complete, all classified as 2c-sequencing with an identical conflict structure: $\mathcal{I}^{\text{scope}}_{\text{MX}} = 3$, $\mathcal{I}^{\text{struct}}_{\text{MX}} = 2$, $\mathcal{I}^{\text{scope}}_{\text{GS}} = 3$, $\mathcal{I}^{\text{struct}}_{\text{GS}} = 2$. All three goals participate in conflicts regardless of grid size (prob03: 15{,}625 operators; prob04: 46{,}656; prob05: 117{,}649). This structural invariance indicates that the conflict topology is determined by the goal specification, not by problem scale.

\subsection{Case Studies}
\label{sec:eval_case_studies}

\subsubsection{Diagnosis prob10: All Three Measures}

Instance prob10 is the only benchmark problem where all three measures are simultaneously non-zero: $(14, 25, 2, 1, 2, 2)$. Of the 24 goals, 14 are unreachable ($\mathcal{I}^{\text{scope}}_{\text{UR}} = 14$), leaving 10 achievable goals. Among these, 2 goals are in a single mutex pair ($\mathcal{I}^{\text{scope}}_{\text{MX}} = 2$, $\mathcal{I}^{\text{struct}}_{\text{MX}} = 1$), and the same 2 goals form 2 ordered sequencing conflicts ($\mathcal{I}^{\text{scope}}_{\text{GS}} = 2$, $\mathcal{I}^{\text{struct}}_{\text{GS}} = 2$). The sequencing structure count of 2 for 2 goals indicates both orderings $(g_1, g_2)$ and $(g_2, g_1)$ are conflicts: achieving either goal permanently prevents the other, consistent with an irreversible exclusion (Category~2c).

This instance demonstrates the complementary diagnostic value of the measures. The unreachability profile identifies 14 goals that cannot be satisfied under any execution trace. The mutex profile identifies 2 additional goals that are individually achievable but cannot coexist in any reachable state. The sequencing profile further specifies that this mutex is caused by irreversible effects rather than by reversible state constraints. A planning engineer could use this decomposition to distinguish between goals requiring new operators (unreachable) and goals requiring operator modification (mutex/sequencing).

\subsubsection{Diagnosis prob16: Dense Conflict Structure}

Instance prob16 exhibits the densest conflict structure in the benchmark suite: $(20, 32, 8, 28, 8, 56)$. Of 36 goals, 20 are unreachable. Among the 16 achievable goals, 8 participate in mutex relationships forming 28 unordered pairs, and the same 8 goals produce 56 ordered sequencing conflicts. The mutex structure count of 28 equals $\binom{8}{2}$, meaning every pair of the 8 conflicting goals is mutex. The sequencing count of 56 equals $8 \times 7$, meaning every ordered pair is a sequencing conflict. This indicates a clique structure: the 8 goals form a complete conflict graph where achieving any one permanently blocks all others.

\subsubsection{Chessboard-pebbling: Structural Invariance}

The three solvable instances of \textit{chessboard-pebbling} (prob03, prob04, prob05) produce identical conflict profiles despite substantially different problem sizes. All three have 3 goals, all 3 participate in mutex and sequencing conflicts, and the structure counts are constant ($\mathcal{I}^{\text{struct}}_{\text{MX}} = 2$, $\mathcal{I}^{\text{struct}}_{\text{GS}} = 2$). The grid sizes grow from 1{,}300 to 4{,}900 propositions and from 15{,}625 to 117{,}649 operators, yet the conflict structure remains invariant. This confirms that the measures capture structural properties of the goal specification rather than artifacts of problem scale.

\subsubsection{Cave-diving prob05: Unreachability in a Resource Domain}

Among the eight completed \textit{cave-diving} instances, prob05 is the only one classified as Category~2a, with profile $(1, 49, 0, 0, 0, 0)$. One of the three goals is unreachable, in contrast to the other seven completed instances where all goals are reachable at $h = 20$. This suggests that prob05 contains a genuinely missing producer (Category~2a), while the other instances have different structural properties that the measures interact with differently depending on the horizon, as analyzed in Section~\ref{sec:eval_horizon}.

\subsubsection{3unsat: Genuine Measure Blind Spot}
\label{sec:3unsat_case_study}

The \textit{3unsat} domain from the Eriksson benchmark collection~\cite{Eriksson2019} encodes unsatisfiable 3-SAT instances as planning problems. Each problem has a set of Boolean variables as operators (assigning true or false) and a set of clauses as goals (each clause is ``solved'' when at least one of its literals is satisfied). All 20 completed instances return the profile $(0, 0, 0, 0, 0, 0)$ with $\mathcal{I}^{\text{struct}}_{\text{UR}} = 0$: not a single proposition is unreachable, and no mutex or sequencing conflicts are detected at any horizon.

This result is fundamentally different from the cave-diving and bottleneck consistent classifications. Testing at $h = 3$ (the minimum horizon needed for a single variable assignment) confirms that the profile remains $(0, 0, 0, 0, 0, 0)$. Each goal clause is individually achievable (by assigning the appropriate variable values), and every pair of clauses can be simultaneously satisfied in some reachable state (by choosing compatible assignments). The unsolvability arises because no single assignment satisfies \emph{all} clauses simultaneously, a combinatorial constraint that pairwise analysis cannot detect.

This represents a genuine limitation of the proposed measures, corresponding to the higher-order goal analysis discussed in Section~\ref{sec:conclusion}. The measures detect pairwise conflicts: for problems with $|G| \leq 2$, zero measures imply solvability. For larger goal sets, pairwise achievability does not guarantee joint achievability. The \textit{3unsat} domain provides the first empirical example of this limitation on benchmark data, complementing the theoretical observation in the future work discussion.

\subsection{Horizon Sensitivity Analysis}
\label{sec:eval_horizon}

The horizon parameter $h$ bounds the number of time steps explored during brave reasoning. This section presents evidence that the choice of horizon significantly affects the computed profiles, revealing a precision-recall tradeoff inherent to horizon-bounded delete-relaxed analysis.

\subsubsection{Mechanism}

Under delete-relaxed state-space exploration, longer horizons allow more propositions to become reachable through longer operator chains. Since delete effects are still applied in the encoding (the relaxation applies to reachability derivation, not state transitions), the interaction between horizon length and conflict detection is non-trivial. Three effects are observed:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Monotonically decreasing unreachability}: As $h$ increases, more propositions become reachable through longer execution traces, so $\mathcal{I}^{\text{scope}}_{\text{UR}}$ decreases monotonically.
    \item \textbf{Non-monotone mutex and sequencing}: Goals that become reachable at longer horizons may initially appear in mutex or sequencing conflicts. At even longer horizons, additional execution traces may provide co-existence or sequencing witnesses that resolve these apparent conflicts.
    \item \textbf{False positives at short horizons}: An insufficient horizon may fail to find witnesses that exist at longer traces, reporting conflicts that do not represent genuine structural limitations.
\end{enumerate}

Table~\ref{tab:horizon_diagnosis} shows the profile of \textit{diagnosis}~prob10 at three horizon values. At $h = 10$, 19 of 24 goals are unreachable and no mutex or sequencing conflicts are detected (the potentially conflicting goals are not yet reachable). At $h = 20$, five additional goals become reachable, two of which are in mutex and sequencing conflict. At $h = 30$, four more goals become reachable and the mutex and sequencing conflicts disappear, as longer traces provide witnesses for goal co-existence.

\subsubsection{False Positive at $h = 10$}

The solvable instance \textit{cave-diving}~\texttt{satprob01} is classified as 2c-sequencing at $h = 10$ with the same conflict structure as the unsolvable instances. Since this problem is known to be solvable, the detected conflicts are false positives: the horizon is too short to discover execution traces that satisfy all goals. At $h = 20$, the same instance is correctly classified as consistent.

This false positive provides empirical justification for the choice of $h = 20$ as the default horizon. A shorter horizon increases the detection rate for unsolvable instances but introduces the risk of incorrectly flagging solvable problems. The default of $h = 20$ avoids this: no false positives are observed among the solvable instances that complete at this horizon.

\subsubsection{Interpretation}

The horizon sensitivity analysis yields three conclusions. First, the classification ``consistent'' at a given horizon means that no structural conflicts are detectable at that exploration depth; it does not constitute a claim of solvability. Second, $h = 20$ represents a practical balance: it avoids false positives on the available solvable instances while detecting genuine unreachability in 25 of 67 unsolvable instances and mutex/sequencing conflicts in 13 additional instances. Third, the nine consistent IPC~2016 classifications at $h = 20$ are not evidence of Category~3 resource constraints. The conflicts exist but are resolved by longer delete-relaxed traces. By contrast, the 20 \textit{3unsat} consistent classifications are horizon-independent (Section~\ref{sec:3unsat_case_study}), representing a different class of undetectable unsolvability.

\subsubsection{Grounding Bottleneck}

The 58\% of instances that exceed the timeout are limited by grounding, not by solving. The timestep-indexed encoding creates ground rules proportional to $|O| \times h$, and Clingo must instantiate choice rules and frame axioms across all time steps before solving begins. This is visible in two domains with extreme grounding growth:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Pegsol-row5}: proposition counts grow as 228, 2{,}772, 13{,}872 across prob01--prob03, with operator counts following a similar exponential trajectory (216, 2{,}744, 13{,}824). After prob03, all instances exceed the timeout.
    \item \textbf{Chessboard-pebbling}: operator counts grow as 15{,}625, 46{,}656, 117{,}649 across prob03--prob05. After prob05, all instances exceed the timeout.
\end{itemize}

This grounding bottleneck is inherent to the ground-and-solve approach used by \textit{clingo}. Lifted \ac{ASP} solving techniques or alternative encoding strategies that avoid full grounding could potentially extend coverage, but such approaches are beyond the scope of this thesis.

\subsection{Discussion}
\label{sec:eval_discussion}

\subsubsection{Empirical Verification of Theoretical Properties}

The benchmark results empirically verify the measure relationships established in Proposition~\ref{prop:measure_relationships}. Across all 67 instances:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Unreachable goals never participate in mutex or sequencing conflicts: in every instance where $\mathcal{I}^{\text{scope}}_{\text{UR}} > 0$, the mutex and sequencing measures operate exclusively on the achievable subset of goals.
    \item Every instance with $\mathcal{I}^{\text{scope}}_{\text{GS}} > 0$ also has $\mathcal{I}^{\text{scope}}_{\text{MX}} > 0$, confirming that sequencing implies mutex. In the benchmark data, $\mathcal{I}^{\text{scope}}_{\text{GS}} = \mathcal{I}^{\text{scope}}_{\text{MX}}$ in all cases, indicating that the benchmark domains do not contain examples of mutex without sequencing.
    \item The scope-structure bounds from Proposition~\ref{prop:scope_structure_bounds} hold in every instance. The bound $\mathcal{I}^{\text{struct}}_{\text{MX}} \leq \binom{\mathcal{I}^{\text{scope}}_{\text{MX}}}{2}$ is tight for \textit{diagnosis}~prob16, where $28 = \binom{8}{2}$.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Diagnostic Utility}

The measures address Research Question~3c by providing three levels of diagnostic information for unsolvable planning problems:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Category classification} distinguishes between unreachability (missing operators or broken dependency chains), mutex conflicts (individually achievable goals that cannot coexist), and sequencing conflicts (irreversible exclusions). This distinction is visible across domains: \textit{diagnosis} exhibits primarily unreachability, \textit{bottleneck} and \textit{chessboard-pebbling} exhibit primarily sequencing.
    \item \textbf{Scope measures} quantify how many goals are affected. For instance, \textit{diagnosis}~prob06 has only 1 unreachable goal out of 7 (14\%), while prob05 has 49 out of 56 (88\%), indicating fundamentally different repair difficulties.
    \item \textbf{Structure measures} quantify the density of conflicts. The contrast between \textit{diagnosis}~prob10 ($\mathcal{I}^{\text{struct}}_{\text{MX}} = 1$, a single conflicting pair) and prob16 ($\mathcal{I}^{\text{struct}}_{\text{MX}} = 28$, a complete conflict graph) illustrates how structure measures distinguish isolated conflicts from systemic ones.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Limitations}

Several limitations must be noted.

\paragraph{Domain Coverage.} The measures are computable for 6 of 15 IPC~2016 domains and 1 additional Eriksson domain, with 67 of 158 instances (42\%) completing within the timeout. The grounding bottleneck restricts applicability to problems of moderate size.

\paragraph{Solvable Instance Validation.} Only two solvable instances complete successfully (\textit{cave-diving}~\texttt{satprob01} and \textit{document-transfer}~\texttt{satprob01}), both correctly classified as consistent at $h = 20$. The \textit{diagnosis} solvable instances fail at the \textit{plasp} translation level due to predicate mismatches, and solvable instances in other domains exceed the timeout. A larger sample of solvable instances would strengthen the validation of the false-positive rate.

\paragraph{Horizon Dependence.} As demonstrated in Section~\ref{sec:eval_horizon}, results depend on the horizon parameter. No theoretically optimal horizon exists: the appropriate value depends on the maximum meaningful plan length for a given domain. The default of $h = 20$ is empirically justified by the absence of false positives, but different domains may benefit from different horizon values.

\paragraph{Consistent Classifications.} Twenty-nine unsolvable instances are classified as consistent at $h = 20$, falling into two distinct categories. The 9 IPC~2016 instances (cave-diving and bottleneck) are horizon-dependent: shorter horizons detect conflicts, but at the cost of false positives. The 20 \textit{3unsat} instances are horizon-independent: the measures return $(0, 0, 0, 0, 0, 0)$ at every horizon, including $h = 3$. This confirms that undetectable unsolvable problems exist, as predicted by the pairwise limitation discussed in Section~\ref{sec:conclusion}. The \textit{3unsat} domain demonstrates that combinatorial unsatisfiability arising from the interaction of more than two goals lies outside the scope of the proposed measures.

\paragraph{Pairwise Limitation.} The \textit{3unsat} results provide the first empirical evidence for the theoretical limitation identified in Section~\ref{sec:conclusion}: pairwise goal analysis cannot detect unsolvability that arises only from the joint interaction of three or more goals. Extending the measures to $k$-tuple analysis would address this gap but at significantly higher computational cost.
