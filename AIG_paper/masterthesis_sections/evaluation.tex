\section{Evaluation}
\label{sec:evaluation}

This chapter evaluates the implemented measures on unsolvable planning benchmarks from the IPC~2016 Unsolvability Competition. The evaluation addresses Research Question~3, asking whether the adapted measures provide practical diagnostic value for understanding and analyzing unsolvable planning problems. Section~\ref{sec:eval_methodology} describes the experimental setup and dataset. Section~\ref{sec:eval_results} presents the benchmark results across seven compatible domains. Section~\ref{sec:eval_case_studies} examines individual problems that illustrate the diagnostic capabilities and limitations of the measures. Section~\ref{sec:eval_horizon} analyzes the sensitivity of results to the horizon parameter, revealing a precision-recall tradeoff that constitutes a key methodological finding. Section~\ref{sec:eval_performance} discusses computational performance and scalability. Section~\ref{sec:eval_discussion} synthesizes the findings and addresses limitations.

\subsection{Evaluation Methodology}
\label{sec:eval_methodology}

\subsubsection{Dataset}

The evaluation uses two benchmark suites. The primary dataset is the IPC~2016 Unsolvability Competition benchmark suite by Muise and Lipovetzky~\cite{Muise2016}, which contains 15 domains of unsolvable planning problems specifically designed for evaluating unsolvability detection techniques. Each domain includes between 15 and 25 unsolvable problem instances of increasing size, and several domains also include solvable instances (\texttt{satprob} files) for validation purposes. The secondary dataset is the unsolvable benchmark collection by Eriksson~\cite{Eriksson2019}, which extends the IPC~2016 domains and adds new domains including \textit{3unsat}, a set of unsatisfiable 3-SAT instances encoded as planning problems.

Of the 15 IPC~2016 domains, 6 are compatible with the implementation pipeline. One additional domain from the Eriksson benchmarks, \textit{3unsat}, is also compatible. Table~\ref{tab:domain_compatibility} summarizes the compatibility status. Two domains (\textit{bag-barman} and \textit{tetris}) use the \texttt{:equality} requirement, which \textit{plasp} does not support, as noted in Section~\ref{sec:pddl_translation}. Seven domains produce grounding programs that exceed the 120-second timeout: \textit{bag-gripper}, \textit{bag-transport}, \textit{over-nomystery}, \textit{over-rovers}, \textit{over-tpp}, \textit{sliding-tiles}, and \textit{pegsol}. The compatible domains span a range of unsolvability types: \textit{diagnosis} exhibits unreachability, mutex, and sequencing conflicts; \textit{bottleneck} and \textit{chessboard-pebbling} exhibit pure mutex and sequencing conflicts without unreachability; \textit{cave-diving}, \textit{document-transfer}, and \textit{pegsol-row5} primarily exhibit unreachability; and \textit{3unsat} encodes combinatorial unsatisfiability outside the scope of the proposed measures.

\subsubsection{Experimental Setup}

All experiments run inside a Docker container with Python~3.12, Clingo~5.8.0, and \textit{plasp}~3.1.1. The horizon parameter is set to $h = 20$ for all primary runs, and each problem has a timeout of 120 seconds. The batch runner (Section~\ref{sec:tooling}) processes all domain/problem pairs in each compatible domain, recording the full diagnostic profile, category classification, problem size metadata (goal, proposition, and operator counts), computation time, and status (OK, TIMEOUT, or ERROR) for each instance. Additional runs at $h = 10$ and $h = 30$ are performed on selected instances for the horizon sensitivity analysis in Section~\ref{sec:eval_horizon}.

\subsection{Case Studies}
\label{sec:eval_case_studies}

\subsubsection{Diagnosis prob10: All Three Measures}

Instance prob10 is the only benchmark problem where all three measures are simultaneously non-zero, with profile $(14, 25, 2, 1, 2, 2)$. Of the 24 goals, 14 are unreachable ($\mathcal{I}^{\text{scope}}_{\text{UR}} = 14$), leaving 10 achievable goals. Among these, 2 goals are in a single mutex pair ($\mathcal{I}^{\text{scope}}_{\text{MX}} = 2$, $\mathcal{I}^{\text{struct}}_{\text{MX}} = 1$), and the same 2 goals form 2 ordered sequencing conflicts ($\mathcal{I}^{\text{scope}}_{\text{GS}} = 2$, $\mathcal{I}^{\text{struct}}_{\text{GS}} = 2$). The sequencing structure count of 2 for 2 goals indicates both orderings $(g_1, g_2)$ and $(g_2, g_1)$ are conflicts, meaning achieving either goal permanently prevents the other. This is consistent with an irreversible exclusion in Category~2c.

This instance demonstrates the complementary diagnostic value of the measures. The unreachability profile identifies 14 goals that cannot be satisfied under any execution trace. The mutex profile identifies 2 additional goals that are individually achievable but cannot coexist in any reachable state. The sequencing profile further specifies that this mutex is caused by irreversible effects rather than by reversible state constraints. A planning engineer could use this decomposition to distinguish between goals requiring new operators (unreachable) and goals requiring operator modification (mutex/sequencing).

\subsubsection{Diagnosis prob16: Dense Conflict Structure}

Instance prob16 exhibits the densest conflict structure in the benchmark suite, with profile $(20, 32, 8, 28, 8, 56)$. Of 36 goals, 20 are unreachable. Among the 16 achievable goals, 8 participate in mutex relationships forming 28 unordered pairs, and the same 8 goals produce 56 ordered sequencing conflicts. The mutex structure count of 28 equals $\binom{8}{2}$, meaning every pair of the 8 conflicting goals is mutex. The sequencing count of 56 equals $8 \times 7$, meaning every ordered pair is a sequencing conflict. This indicates a clique structure where the 8 goals form a complete conflict graph and achieving any one permanently blocks all others.

\subsubsection{Chessboard-pebbling: Structural Invariance}

The three successfully computed instances of \textit{chessboard-pebbling} (prob03, prob04, prob05) produce identical conflict profiles despite substantially different problem sizes. All three have 3 goals, all 3 participate in mutex and sequencing conflicts, and the structure counts are constant ($\mathcal{I}^{\text{struct}}_{\text{MX}} = 2$, $\mathcal{I}^{\text{struct}}_{\text{GS}} = 2$). The grid sizes grow from 1{,}300 to 4{,}900 propositions and from 15{,}625 to 117{,}649 operators, yet the conflict structure remains invariant. This confirms that the measures capture structural properties of the goal specification rather than artifacts of problem scale.

\subsubsection{Cave-diving prob05: Unreachability in a Resource Domain}

Among the nine completed \textit{cave-diving} instances, prob05 is the only one classified as Category~2a, with profile $(1, 49, 0, 0, 0, 0)$. One of the three goals is unreachable, in contrast to the other eight completed instances where all goals are reachable at $h = 20$. This suggests that prob05 contains a genuinely missing producer (Category~2a), while the other instances have no detectable pairwise structural conflicts, placing their unsolvability outside the scope of the proposed measures.

\subsubsection{3unsat: Genuine Measure Blind Spot}
\label{sec:3unsat_case_study}

The \textit{3unsat} domain from the Eriksson benchmark collection~\cite{Eriksson2019} encodes unsatisfiable 3-SAT instances as planning problems. Each problem has a set of Boolean variables as operators (assigning true or false) and a set of clauses as goals (each clause is ``solved'' when at least one of its literals is satisfied). All 20 completed instances return the profile $(0, 0, 0, 0, 0, 0)$ with $\mathcal{I}^{\text{struct}}_{\text{UR}} = 0$: not a single proposition is unreachable, and no mutex or sequencing conflicts are detected at any horizon.

This result is fundamentally different from the cave-diving and bottleneck consistent classifications. Testing at $h = 3$ (the minimum horizon needed for a single variable assignment) confirms that the profile remains $(0, 0, 0, 0, 0, 0)$. Each goal clause is individually achievable (by assigning the appropriate variable values), and every pair of clauses can be simultaneously satisfied in some reachable state (by choosing compatible assignments). The unsolvability arises because no single assignment satisfies \emph{all} clauses simultaneously, a combinatorial constraint that pairwise analysis cannot detect.

This represents a genuine limitation of the proposed measures, corresponding to the higher-order goal analysis discussed in Section~\ref{sec:conclusion}. The measures detect pairwise conflicts. For problems with $|G| \leq 2$, zero measures imply solvability. For larger goal sets, pairwise achievability does not guarantee joint achievability. The \textit{3unsat} domain provides the first empirical example of this limitation on benchmark data, complementing the theoretical observation in the future work discussion.

\subsection{Horizon Sensitivity Analysis}
\label{sec:eval_horizon}

The horizon parameter $h$ bounds the number of time steps explored during brave reasoning. This section presents evidence that the choice of horizon significantly affects the computed profiles, revealing a precision-recall tradeoff inherent to horizon-bounded brave reasoning.

\subsubsection{Mechanism}

Under the horizon-bounded brave reasoning approach, longer horizons allow more propositions to become reachable through longer operator chains. Since the encoding explores nondeterministic execution traces up to the given horizon bound, the interaction between horizon length and conflict detection is non-trivial. The formal properties established in Proposition~\ref{prop:horizon_convergence} predict three effects, all of which are observed empirically:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Monotonically decreasing unreachability}: As $h$ increases, $S^h_{\text{reach}} \subseteq S^{h+1}_{\text{reach}}$ by Proposition~\ref{prop:horizon_convergence}, so $\mathcal{I}^{\text{scope}}_{\text{UR}}(\Pi, h)$ is monotonically non-increasing in $h$.
    \item \textbf{Non-monotone mutex and sequencing}: As noted in Section~\ref{sec:horizon_bounded}, $\mathcal{I}^{\text{scope}}_{\text{MX}}(\Pi, h)$ and $\mathcal{I}^{\text{scope}}_{\text{GS}}(\Pi, h)$ are not monotone in $h$, because increasing $h$ simultaneously makes new goals reachable, which may introduce apparent conflicts, and provides longer witness traces, which may resolve previously reported conflicts.
    \item \textbf{False positives at short horizons}: An insufficient horizon may fail to find witnesses that exist at longer traces, reporting conflicts that do not represent genuine structural limitations. These are correct values of $\mathcal{I}(\Pi, h)$ for the given $h$ but do not reflect the converged values $\mathcal{I}(\Pi, h^*)$.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Evidence: Diagnosis prob10}

Table~\ref{tab:horizon_diagnosis} shows the profile of \textit{diagnosis}~prob10 at three horizon values. At $h = 10$, $\mathcal{I}^{\text{scope}}_{\text{UR}}(\text{prob10}, 10) = 19$ and no mutex or sequencing conflicts are detected because the potentially conflicting goals are not yet reachable. At $h = 20$, five additional goals become reachable, two of which appear in mutex and sequencing conflict with $\mathcal{I}^{\text{scope}}_{\text{MX}}(\text{prob10}, 20) = 2$ and $\mathcal{I}^{\text{scope}}_{\text{GS}}(\text{prob10}, 20) = 2$. At $h = 30$, three more goals become reachable and the mutex and sequencing measures return to zero with $\mathcal{I}^{\text{scope}}_{\text{MX}}(\text{prob10}, 30) = 0$. This demonstrates the non-monotonicity predicted in Section~\ref{sec:horizon_bounded}. The conflicts reported at $h = 20$ are horizon artifacts rather than genuine structural limitations, as longer traces provide witnesses for goal co-existence. The prob10 case illustrates that the horizon sensitivity is not limited to the distinction between ``consistent'' and ``conflicting'' classifications. Even non-zero conflict detections can be artifacts of an intermediate horizon that has not yet converged.

\subsubsection{Interpretation}

The horizon sensitivity analysis yields three conclusions. First, each computed profile is a correct value of $\mathcal{I}(\Pi, h)$ for the given horizon $h$; the classification ``consistent'' at a given horizon means that no pairwise structural conflicts are detectable at that exploration depth, not that the converged measures $\mathcal{I}(\Pi, h^*)$ are zero. Second, $h = 20$ represents a practical balance: it avoids false positives on the available solvable instances while detecting genuine unreachability in 25 of 68 unsolvable instances and mutex/sequencing conflicts in 13 additional instances. Third, the ten consistent IPC~2016 classifications at $h = 20$ are not evidence of missed pairwise conflicts. A shorter horizon of $h = 10$ produces apparent conflict detections on these instances, but the identical detections on the solvable \texttt{satprob01} demonstrate that these are artifacts of insufficient exploration depth rather than genuine structural limitations. The unsolvability of these instances, like that of the 20 horizon-independent \textit{3unsat} instances (Section~\ref{sec:3unsat_case_study}), lies outside the scope of pairwise analysis.

\subsubsection{Grounding Bottleneck}

The 57\% of instances that exceed the timeout are limited by grounding, not by solving. The timestep-indexed encoding creates ground rules proportional to $|O| \times h$, and Clingo must instantiate choice rules and frame axioms across all time steps before solving begins. This is visible in two domains with extreme grounding growth:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Pegsol-row5}: proposition counts grow as 228, 2{,}772, 13{,}872 across prob01--prob03, with operator counts following a similar exponential trajectory (216, 2{,}744, 13{,}824). After prob03, all instances exceed the timeout.
    \item \textbf{Chessboard-pebbling}: operator counts grow as 15{,}625, 46{,}656, 117{,}649 across prob03--prob05. After prob05, all instances exceed the timeout.
\end{itemize}

This grounding bottleneck is inherent to the ground-and-solve approach used by \textit{clingo}. Lifted \ac{ASP} solving techniques or alternative encoding strategies that avoid full grounding could potentially extend coverage, but such approaches are beyond the scope of this thesis.

\subsection{Discussion}
\label{sec:eval_discussion}

\subsubsection{Empirical Verification of Theoretical Properties}

The benchmark results empirically verify all measure relationships from Proposition~\ref{prop:measure_relationships} and all scope-structure bounds from Proposition~\ref{prop:scope_structure_bounds}. Across all 68 computed instances, unreachable goals never participate in mutex or sequencing conflicts, and every instance with $\mathcal{I}^{\text{scope}}_{\text{GS}} > 0$ also has $\mathcal{I}^{\text{scope}}_{\text{MX}} > 0$. The upper bound $\mathcal{I}^{\text{struct}}_{\text{MX}} \leq \binom{\mathcal{I}^{\text{scope}}_{\text{MX}}}{2}$ is achieved tightly for \textit{diagnosis}~prob16, where $28 = \binom{8}{2}$.

\subsubsection{Diagnostic Utility}

The measures address Research Question~3c by providing three levels of diagnostic information for unsolvable planning problems. At the coarsest level, category classification distinguishes unreachability from mutex and sequencing conflicts. This distinction is visible across domains, as \textit{diagnosis} exhibits primarily unreachability while \textit{bottleneck} and \textit{chessboard-pebbling} exhibit primarily sequencing. At a finer level, scope measures quantify how many goals are affected by each conflict type. For instance, \textit{diagnosis}~prob06 has only 1 unreachable goal out of 7, representing 14\% of goals, while prob05 has 49 out of 56, representing 88\%, indicating fundamentally different repair difficulties. At the finest level, structure measures quantify the density of conflicts among affected goals. The contrast between \textit{diagnosis}~prob10 with $\mathcal{I}^{\text{struct}}_{\text{MX}} = 1$ and prob16 with $\mathcal{I}^{\text{struct}}_{\text{MX}} = 28$ illustrates how structure measures distinguish isolated conflicts from complete conflict graphs.

\subsubsection{Limitations}

Several limitations must be noted.

\paragraph{Domain Coverage.} The measures are computable for 6 of 15 IPC~2016 domains and 1 additional Eriksson domain, with 68 of 158 instances completing within the timeout. The grounding bottleneck restricts applicability to problems of moderate size.

\paragraph{Solvable Instance Validation.} Only two solvable instances complete successfully, \textit{cave-diving}~\texttt{satprob01} and \textit{document-transfer}~\texttt{satprob01}, both correctly classified as consistent at $h = 20$. The remaining solvable instances fail for two distinct reasons. The \textit{diagnosis} solvable instances and several \textit{cave-diving} solvable instances fail at the \textit{plasp} translation level due to predicate mismatches, which is a pipeline incompatibility rather than a scalability limitation. Solvable instances in other domains exceed the 120-second timeout. A larger sample of solvable instances would strengthen the validation of the false-positive rate.

\paragraph{Horizon Dependence.} As demonstrated in Section~\ref{sec:eval_horizon}, results depend on the horizon parameter. No theoretically optimal horizon exists, and the appropriate value depends on the maximum meaningful plan length for a given domain. The default of $h = 20$ is empirically justified by the absence of false positives, but different domains may benefit from different horizon values.

\paragraph{Consistent Classifications.} Thirty unsolvable instances are classified as consistent at $h = 20$. As shown in Section~\ref{sec:eval_horizon}, the 10 IPC~2016 instances exhibit horizon-dependent behavior where shorter horizons produce spurious detections indistinguishable from false positives on solvable instances. The 20 \textit{3unsat} instances are horizon-independent (Section~\ref{sec:3unsat_case_study}). Both groups confirm that unsolvable problems exist whose unsolvability lies outside the scope of pairwise analysis.

\paragraph{Pairwise Limitation.} The \textit{3unsat} domain (Section~\ref{sec:3unsat_case_study}) provides the first empirical evidence that pairwise goal analysis cannot detect unsolvability arising from the joint interaction of three or more goals. Extending the measures to $k$-tuple analysis would address this gap but at significantly higher computational cost.
