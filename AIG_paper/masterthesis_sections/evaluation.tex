\section{Evaluation}
\label{sec:evaluation}

This chapter presents a comprehensive evaluation of the adapted inconsistency measures and their implementation, assessing both computational feasibility and practical diagnostic value. The evaluation demonstrates the effectiveness of the approach through systematic experimentation on benchmark planning problems and analysis of the diagnostic insights provided.

\subsection{Experimental Framework and Methodology}

This section establishes the experimental framework used for evaluation, including the selection of benchmark planning problems from established collections, the design of test scenarios covering different types of unsolvable planning instances, and the methodology for measuring both computational performance and diagnostic utility.

\subsection{Benchmark Problem Selection}

The evaluation employs planning problems from established benchmark repositories to ensure coverage of different domain characteristics, problem complexities, and types of unsolvability.

\subsubsection{Primary Benchmark Source}

The primary evaluation dataset is the curated collection of unsolvable PDDL benchmarks~\cite{Eriksson2019}, available through Zenodo. This collection combines benchmarks from the Unsolvability IPC 2016~\cite{Lipovetzky2016} and the Saarland University unsatisfiable benchmark collection, with all solvable problems removed to provide a focused dataset of exclusively unsolvable instances. The repository includes five diverse planning domains:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Bottleneck}: Resource allocation with capacity constraints
    \item \textbf{Pegsol}: Peg solitaire puzzles with dead-end states
    \item \textbf{Nomystery}: Transportation with fuel limitations
    \item \textbf{Rovers}: Planetary exploration with spatial and resource constraints
    \item \textbf{TPP}: Market logistics with routing and purchasing constraints
\end{itemize}

These domains represent different categories of planning challenges and constraint interactions, enabling evaluation across varied structural characteristics.

\subsubsection{Complementary Benchmark Sources}

Additional benchmark repositories complement the primary dataset and enable comparison with broader planning literature:

\paragraph{Planning.domains API} The planning.domains repository~\cite{Muise2020} provides programmatic access to PDDL benchmark collections from IPC competitions spanning 1998 to present. Selected instances validate that adapted measures correctly distinguish solvable from unsolvable problems.

\paragraph{Potassco PDDL Instances} The potassco/pddl-instances repository~\cite{Gebser2024} maintains PDDL benchmark instances from IPC 1998-2014, encompassing 87 domains and 291 problem variants with documented characteristics for cross-domain comparative analysis.

\paragraph{AI-Planning Classical Domains} The classical-domains collection~\cite{APO2024} provides reference benchmarks for validating measure implementations on well-studied planning problems.

\subsubsection{Selection Criteria}

Benchmark selection follows systematic criteria:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Unsolvability Focus}: Confirmed unsolvable instances for direct assessment of diagnostic capabilities
    \item \textbf{Domain Diversity}: Structurally different domains to test measure generalizability
    \item \textbf{Complexity Range}: Problems spanning different sizes for scalability assessment
    \item \textbf{Conflict Type Variety}: Different sources of unsolvability (goal conflicts, resource constraints, action precondition conflicts, dead-end states)
    \item \textbf{Established Provenance}: Widely-recognized benchmarks enabling comparison and reproducibility
\end{enumerate}

The experimental framework integrates benchmarks with plasp 3~\cite{Dimopoulos2019}, converting PDDL specifications to ASP facts for processing by the implemented measure encodings.

This section evaluates the computational feasibility and scalability of the implemented measures on problems of varying complexity. Performance metrics include computation time, memory usage, and scalability limits, with analysis of how problem characteristics affect computational requirements.

This includes evaluation of how effectively the measures identify conflict sources, distinguish between different types of planning failures, and provide meaningful quantitative assessments.

\subsection{Comparative Analysis}

This section presents comparative analysis between different adapted measures, examining their relative strengths and weaknesses for various types of planning problems. The comparison considers both computational efficiency and diagnostic discriminative power.

\subsection{Case Studies}

Detailed case studies demonstrate the application of the measures to representative unsolvable planning problems, showing how the quantitative diagnostics help identify conflict sources and suggest potential repair strategies. The case studies cover different planning domains and conflict types.

This section addresses the validation of results through comparison with existing diagnostic approaches where available, verification of measure properties through constructed test cases, and assessment of the consistency and reliability of the diagnostic outputs.
